Changes in gut microbiome composition and function during fasting

The first study I realised with the Buchinger Wilhelmi clinics was a study of changes in gut microbiome composition during fasting. Analysing the gut microbiome data is the initial reason I was hired. Despite the role that the human gut microbiota plays in health and diseases, little is known about its composition and functional capacity during prolonged fasting when the external nutrient supply is reduced or suppressed. We analysed the effects of a 10-d periodic fasting on the faecal microbiota of fifteen healthy men (Mesnage et al., 2019).
Even when the flow of nutrient is stopped during long-term fasting, the gut microbiota does not fully disappear, though its profile changes dramatically. The species that increase during fasting are those with the highest proteolytic activity; they use host-derived compounds such as mucus or desquamated cells as a source of energy. Fasting causes a decrease in the relative abundance of bacteria that degrade dietary polysaccharides, such as Lachnospiraceae and Ruminococcaceae. The microbiota in long-term fasting humans is very similar to that in hibernating animals.
Changes in bacteria profiles were associated with serum glucose and faecal branched-chain amino acids (BCAA), suggesting that fasting-induced changes in the gut microbiota are associated with energy metabolism. These effects were reversed after 3 months. We also monitored intestinal permeability and inflammatory status. IL-6, IL-10, interferon γ and TNFα levels increased when food was reintroduced, suggesting a reactivation of the postprandial immune response. This study on healthy subjects lays the foundation for further investigations on the impact of fasting on the microbiota and their relationship with metabolic and inflammatory mediators in diet-related chronic diseases.

Fasting in the treatment of hypertension.

The management of high blood pressure is an important public health issue because it is one of the leading risk factors for cardiovascular diseases. We investigated daily blood pressure changes during fasting periods ranging from 4 to 41 days in a cohort of 1610 subjects, including 920 normotensive, 313 hypertensive nonmedicated, and 377 hypertensive medicated individuals (Grundler et al., 2020). Blood pressure decreased from 126/81 to 120/78 mmHg. This reduction reached 24.7/13.1 mm Hg for hypertensive nonmedicated subjects with the highest blood pressure (>160/100 mm Hg). In the normotensive group, blood pressure was relatively unchanged and only decreased moderately by 3/2 mmHg. Interestingly, we documented an increase of 6/2 mmHg in a subgroup of 69 female subjects with blood pressure <100/60 mm Hg. Hypertensive nonmedicated as well as medicated subjects normalized their BP during long‐term fasting; remarkably, two thirds of the medicated subjects could reduce their antihypertensive medication. Baseline metabolic parameters, such as body mass index and glucose levels, as well as age, can be used to predict the amplitude of the blood pressure decrease during fasting with a machine learning model. Fasting appears to be a promising nonpharmacological complementary approach in the treatment of hypertension.
